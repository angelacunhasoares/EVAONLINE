\begin{abstract}
Reference evapotranspiration (ET\textsubscript{0}) is a critical parameter for
irrigation scheduling, hydrological modelling, and climate impact assessment,
yet its accurate estimation remains challenging in regions with sparse
meteorological networks. This paper presents EVAonline, an open-source,
web-based platform that improves ET\textsubscript{0} estimation through a
two-stage data fusion strategy: (i)~region-adaptive weighted averaging of six
public climate APIs (NASA POWER, Open-Meteo Archive, Open-Meteo Forecast, MET
Norway, NWS Daily Forecast, and NWS Stations), followed by (ii)~an adaptive
one-dimensional Kalman filter constrained by 30-year regional monthly
climatology (1991--2020 normals and P01/P99 percentile bounds). The fused
meteorological variables serve as input to a complete FAO-56 Penman-Monteith
implementation. The platform was validated against the BR-DWGD gridded
reference dataset at 17 data-scarce locations across the MATOPIBA agricultural
frontier in Brazil, yielding a Kling-Gupta Efficiency (KGE) of $0.814 \pm
0.053$, Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE) of $0.676 \pm 0.085$, and Mean
Absolute Error (MAE) of \SI{0.423}{mm.d^{-1}}, with a mean bias (PBIAS) of
only $0.71 \pm 0.53\%$. The fusion reduced RMSE by 26\% relative to the best
individual source (NASA POWER) and by 38\% relative to Open-Meteo Archive.
EVAonline is deployed as 13 Docker-containerized microservices following a
hexagonal (Ports \& Adapters) architecture, requires no local installation or
API keys, and provides three operational modes (historical, recent, and
forecast), interactive dashboards, CSV/Excel data exports via email, and a
bilingual interface (English/Portuguese). The source code is available under
the AGPL-3.0 license at \url{https://github.com/angelacunhasoares/EVAONLINE},
and the complete validation dataset is published on Zenodo.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Reference evapotranspiration (ET\textsubscript{0}) is a fundamental parameter
in hydrology, agricultural water management, irrigation scheduling, and
climate impact assessment. The FAO-56 Penman-Monteith (PM) equation
\citep{Allen1998} is the internationally recommended standard for
ET\textsubscript{0} estimation, requiring complete meteorological datasets
including maximum and minimum air temperature, relative humidity, wind speed,
solar radiation, and atmospheric pressure. Despite its widespread adoption, the
application of the FAO-56 PM equation is frequently constrained by the
availability, quality, and spatial density of meteorological observations,
particularly in developing countries and remote agricultural regions.

A persistent challenge in ET\textsubscript{0} estimation is the reliability of
input meteorological data in regions with sparse weather station networks.
Gridded reanalysis and satellite-derived datasets---such as NASA POWER
\citep{Stackhouse2018}, ERA5 \citep{Hersbach2020}, and numerical weather
prediction (NWP) outputs---offer global coverage but exhibit source-specific
biases and uncertainties that vary by variable, region, and season. Using a
single data source introduces systematic errors that propagate through the
nonlinear PM equation, potentially leading to significant ET\textsubscript{0}
estimation errors \citep{Bai2010, Blankenau2020, Paredes2020}. Multiple
studies have demonstrated that no single gridded product consistently
outperforms others across all meteorological variables and climatic regions
\citep{Monteiro2022, Sheffield2006}, suggesting that data fusion approaches
may yield more robust estimates.

The MATOPIBA region---comprising the Brazilian states of Maranh\~ao (MA),
Tocantins (TO), Piau\'i (PI), and western Bahia (BA)---exemplifies this
challenge. MATOPIBA is one of the most dynamic agricultural frontiers in the
world, responsible for approximately 10\% of Brazilian grain production, with
cropland area expanding at rates exceeding 3\% per year
\citep{Miranda2014, Bolfe2016}. The region's tropical climate (K\"oppen
Aw/BSh), with a pronounced dry season from May to September and mean annual
temperatures of 25--28~\textdegree{}C, makes accurate
ET\textsubscript{0} estimation critical for irrigation planning and water
resource management. However, MATOPIBA has one of the lowest densities of
meteorological stations in Brazil \citep{Xavier2016}, creating a significant
gap between the precision required for irrigation management and the
observational data available.

Several software tools exist for ET\textsubscript{0} computation: CROPWAT~8.0
\citep{cropwat} and the ETo Calculator \citep{etocalculator} from FAO are
widely used desktop applications that implement the FAO-56 PM equation but
require manual data input and offer no multi-source fusion capabilities;
Ref-ET \citep{refet} supports multiple equation formulations but is a
commercial desktop product; PyETo \citep{pyeto} is an open-source Python
library that provides programmatic access but requires programming knowledge;
and OpenET \citep{openet} is a web-based platform focused on actual
evapotranspiration from satellite imagery over the contiguous United States.
None of these tools combines automatic multi-source data acquisition, optimal
data fusion with quantified uncertainty, web-based access requiring no
installation, and global coverage in a single, freely available platform.

This paper presents EVAonline, a web-based platform that addresses these
limitations through an integrated approach combining: (a)~automatic data
acquisition from six public climate APIs covering both historical reanalysis
and real-time forecast products; (b)~a two-stage data fusion strategy
consisting of region-adaptive weighted averaging followed by adaptive Kalman
filtering with climatological constraints; (c)~a complete FAO-56
Penman-Monteith implementation with water deficit analysis; and (d)~a
production-ready web interface accessible through any modern browser,
eliminating the need for local software installation, programming knowledge,
or manual data preprocessing. EVAonline is implemented as 13 Docker-containerized
microservices following a hexagonal architecture, ensuring modularity, testability,
and straightforward deployment.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows:
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the system architecture, data sources,
fusion methodology, and ET\textsubscript{0} calculation procedures;
Section~\ref{sec:results_discussion} presents the validation results against
the BR-DWGD reference dataset, compares EVAonline with existing tools, and
discusses strengths and limitations; and Section~\ref{sec:conclusions}
summarizes the main contributions and outlines future development directions.

\subsection{System Architecture}
\label{subsec:architecture}

EVAonline is implemented as a distributed microservice architecture comprising
13 containerized services orchestrated through Docker Compose
(Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}). The system follows a hexagonal (Ports \&
Adapters) architecture pattern \citep{Cockburn2005} that enforces strict
separation between domain logic, external service integration, and
infrastructure concerns.

The architecture is organized into three concentric layers:

\textbf{Domain core.} The innermost layer contains all business logic with zero
external I/O dependencies, including the FAO-56 PM calculation service
(\texttt{EToCalculationService}), the multi-source data fusion engine
(\texttt{ClimateFusion}), and the adaptive Kalman filter
(\texttt{AdaptiveKalmanFilter}). This layer operates exclusively on in-memory
data structures (Pandas DataFrames and NumPy arrays), ensuring testability and
portability.

\textbf{Adapter layer.} Six climate data adapters implement a standardized
interface for external API communication. Each adapter comprises a client class
(handling HTTP requests, retries, and response parsing) and a synchronization
adapter (transforming source-specific schemas into the standardized internal
representation). A \texttt{ClimateClientFactory} provides dependency injection,
enabling runtime selection of data sources based on geographic region and
operational mode.

\textbf{Infrastructure layer.} External services---Redis 7.x for caching and
message brokering, PostgreSQL 16 with PostGIS for spatial data persistence,
and Celery 5.x for distributed task processing---are accessed exclusively
through infrastructure adapters, ensuring the domain core remains independent
of specific technology choices.

The frontend is built with Dash~2.x \citep{plotly} and communicates with the
FastAPI backend \citep{Ramirez2018} exclusively through RESTful HTTP endpoints
and WebSocket connections. Nginx serves as a reverse proxy handling SSL/TLS
termination, rate limiting (30~requests/s general, 5~requests/s for
computations), and static asset serving. Application monitoring is provided by
a Prometheus + Grafana stack that collects request latency, error rates, and
calculation throughput metrics, while Celery task monitoring is provided
through Flower.

Deployment requires a single \texttt{docker compose up} command, which
initializes the complete platform including database creation, schema migration
via Alembic, seed data loading, and service health verification. The
multi-stage Docker build produces a production image of approximately 450~MB.

Site elevation, required for the barometric estimation of atmospheric pressure,
is obtained from the Open-Elevation API (SRTM 30\,m resolution). When the API
is unavailable, a latitude-based empirical function provides a fallback
estimate. Elevation values are cached in Redis with a 24-hour time-to-live
(TTL). Atmospheric pressure is estimated from elevation using the barometric
formula \citep{Allen1998}:

\begin{equation}
P = 101.3 \left(\frac{293 - 0.0065\,z}{293}\right)^{5.26}
\label{eq:pressure}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $z$ (m) is the site elevation and $P$ (kPa) is the atmospheric
pressure.

All wind speed measurements are adjusted to the standard reference height of
2~m using the FAO-56 logarithmic wind profile equation \citep{Allen1998}:

\begin{equation}
u_2 = u_z \cdot \frac{4.87}{\ln(67.8\,z - 5.42)}
\label{eq:wind}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $u_z$ (m\,s\textsuperscript{$-1$}) is the wind speed measured
at height $z$ (m). For NASA POWER and Open-Meteo ($z = 10$~m), the correction
factor is 0.748.


\subsection{Data Quality Control and Multi-Source Fusion}
\label{subsec:fusion}

The EVAonline data processing pipeline employs a two-stage fusion strategy:
region-adaptive weighted averaging of standardized multi-source data, followed
by adaptive Kalman filtering constrained by 30-year climatological priors
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fusion}). Prior to fusion, all data undergo a multi-layer
quality control procedure.

\subsubsection*{Quality control}

Each source's data undergoes three sequential quality control steps before
entering the fusion stage:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Physical range validation.} Measurements are checked against
  region-specific physically plausible bounds derived from WMO records and
  the Brazilian weather gridded dataset \citep{Xavier2016, Xavier2022}. For
  Brazilian locations, tighter bounds are applied (e.g., temperature
  $-30$~to~50~\textdegree{}C, precipitation 0--450~mm\,d\textsuperscript{$-1$},
  solar radiation 0--40~MJ\,m\textsuperscript{$-2$}\,d\textsuperscript{$-1$}).
  Global bounds are wider to accommodate extreme climates (e.g., temperature
  $-90$~to~60~\textdegree{}C). Values exceeding these ranges are set to NaN.

\item \textbf{Outlier detection.} An adaptive interquartile range (IQR) method
  identifies statistical outliers with a configurable IQR factor. A maximum of
  5\% of records may be flagged as outliers per variable to prevent excessive
  data removal. Solar radiation is additionally validated against
  extraterrestrial radiation ($R_a$) to ensure $0.03\,R_a \leq R_s \leq R_a$.

\item \textbf{Circuit-breaker mechanism.} A per-source quality score is
  computed as the product of completeness (fraction of non-null values) and
  validity (fraction within physical bounds) for each variable. Sources with an
  average quality score below 60\% are automatically excluded from the fusion
  for the current request, preventing degraded data from contaminating the
  fused output.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Stage 1: Region-adaptive weighted averaging}

For each day $k$ and each meteorological variable $j$, the Stage~1 fused
estimate is computed as a normalized weighted average of all healthy sources
providing valid data:

\begin{equation}
\hat{z}_{k,j} = \frac{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}_{k,j}} w_i \cdot z_{k,i,j}}{\sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}_{k,j}} w_i}
\label{eq:weighted_avg}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathcal{S}_{k,j}$ is the set of sources providing a valid
measurement for variable $j$ on day $k$, $z_{k,i,j}$ is the observation from
source $i$, and $w_i$ are region-dependent source weights (Table~\ref{tab:weights}).
When only a single source provides data for a given variable on a given day,
that source's value is used directly ($w_i = 1$). The weights are assigned
uniformly across all meteorological variables for each source, reflecting the
overall reliability and resolution of each data provider within its geographic
domain.

After weighted averaging, the fused values are clipped to physical bounds and
gaps of up to three consecutive days are filled by linear interpolation,
constrained within the variable-specific physical limits to prevent
extrapolation artifacts. Days with fewer than four valid variables are discarded.

\subsubsection*{Stage 2: Adaptive Kalman smoothing}

Following the weighted averaging stage, an adaptive one-dimensional scalar
Kalman filter \citep{Kalman1960} is applied independently to precipitation and
ET\textsubscript{0} to provide temporal smoothing informed by climatological
priors. This stage corrects for residual day-to-day noise in the fused
estimates, particularly during transition seasons when inter-source variability
is highest.

For each filtered variable $v \in \{\text{precipitation}, \text{ET}_0\}$ and
each calendar month $m \in \{1, \ldots, 12\}$, a separate filter instance is
initialized using climatological statistics from the 1991--2020 reference
period:

\begin{itemize}
\item Prior state estimate: $\hat{x}_0 = \mu_m$ (climatological monthly normal)
\item Prior error variance: $P_0 = \sigma_m^2$ (climatological monthly variance)
\item Process noise: $Q_m = 0.08\,\sigma_m^2$
\item Base measurement noise: $R_{\text{base}} = 0.3025$
\end{itemize}

The state equation follows a random walk model appropriate for day-to-day
meteorological persistence:

\begin{equation}
x_k = x_{k-1} + w_{k-1}, \quad w_{k-1} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q_m)
\label{eq:kalman_state}
\end{equation}

\noindent with the observation model:

\begin{equation}
z_k = x_k + v_k, \quad v_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R_k)
\label{eq:kalman_obs}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $z_k$ is the Stage~1 fused estimate for day $k$, and $R_k$ is
an adaptive measurement noise variance that increases when observations deviate
from climatologically expected ranges. Specifically:

\begin{equation}
R_k =
\begin{cases}
500\,R_{\text{base}} & \text{if } z_k < 0.8\,\text{P01}_m \text{ or } z_k > 1.25\,\text{P99}_m \\
50\,R_{\text{base}} & \text{if } z_k < \text{P01}_m \text{ or } z_k > \text{P99}_m \\
R_{\text{base}} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:adaptive_R}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{P01}_m$ and $\text{P99}_m$ are the 1st and 99th
percentile bounds of the local climatological distribution for calendar
month~$m$. This three-tier scheme effectively downweights observations that
lie outside the climatologically plausible range, with extreme outliers (beyond
$0.8 \times \text{P01}$ or $1.25 \times \text{P99}$) receiving 500-fold noise
inflation—retaining their partial information rather than discarding them
entirely.

The standard Kalman prediction and update equations are applied:

\begin{align}
\hat{x}_k^{-} &= \hat{x}_{k-1}^{+} \label{eq:kalman_predict_x}\\
P_k^{-} &= P_{k-1}^{+} + Q_m \label{eq:kalman_predict_P}\\
K_k &= \frac{P_k^{-}}{P_k^{-} + R_k} \label{eq:kalman_gain}\\
\hat{x}_k^{+} &= \hat{x}_k^{-} + K_k\,(z_k - \hat{x}_k^{-}) \label{eq:kalman_update_x}\\
P_k^{+} &= (1 - K_k)\,P_k^{-} \label{eq:kalman_update_P}
\end{align}

\noindent where $K_k$ is the Kalman gain, $\hat{x}_k^{-}$ and $P_k^{-}$ are
the prior (predicted) state and error variance, and $\hat{x}_k^{+}$ and
$P_k^{+}$ are the posterior (updated) estimates.

Additionally, the process noise $Q_m$ adapts dynamically to track rapid
changes: when the current innovation $|z_k - \hat{x}_k^{-}|$ exceeds 1.5
times the previous innovation, $Q_m$ is inflated by a factor of~1.8 (capped at
$0.5\,\sigma_m^2$), temporarily increasing the filter's responsiveness before
reverting to the baseline value.

The climatological priors are derived from a curated database of 27 reference
cities (17 in Brazil and 10 global sites), each containing monthly statistics
(normal, standard deviation, and P01--P99 percentiles) computed from the
1991--2020 period of the BR-DWGD dataset \citep{Xavier2016, Xavier2022}. For a
given query location, the system identifies the nearest reference city within
200~km in the same hemisphere and geographic region. Locations without a nearby
reference ($>$200~km) fall back to a simplified Kalman filter with fixed
parameters, which provides temporal smoothing without climatological
constraints.


\subsection{FAO-56 Penman-Monteith ET\textsubscript{0} Calculation}
\label{subsec:eto}

Following data fusion, the daily reference evapotranspiration is computed using
the FAO-56 Penman-Monteith equation \citep{Allen1998}:

\begin{equation}
\text{ET}_0 = \frac{0.408\,\Delta\,(R_n - G) + \gamma\,\dfrac{900}{T_{\text{mean}}+273}\,u_2\,(e_s - e_a)}{\Delta + \gamma\,(1 + 0.34\,u_2)}
\label{eq:fao56}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{ET}_0$ is the reference evapotranspiration
(mm\,d\textsuperscript{$-1$}), $R_n$ is the net radiation at the crop surface
(MJ\,m\textsuperscript{$-2$}\,d\textsuperscript{$-1$}), $G$ is the soil heat
flux density (MJ\,m\textsuperscript{$-2$}\,d\textsuperscript{$-1$}, assumed
zero for daily time steps), $T_{\text{mean}}$ is the mean daily air temperature
at 2~m height (\textdegree{}C), $u_2$ is the wind speed at 2~m height
(m\,s\textsuperscript{$-1$}), $e_s$ is the saturation vapour pressure (kPa),
$e_a$ is the actual vapour pressure (kPa), $\Delta$ is the slope of the
saturation vapour pressure curve (kPa\,\textdegree{}C\textsuperscript{$-1$}),
and $\gamma$ is the psychrometric constant
(kPa\,\textdegree{}C\textsuperscript{$-1$}).

The intermediate quantities are computed as specified in \citet{Allen1998}:

\textbf{Saturation vapour pressure:}
\begin{equation}
e_s = \frac{e^\circ(T_{\max}) + e^\circ(T_{\min})}{2}, \quad
e^\circ(T) = 0.6108\,\exp\!\left[\frac{17.27\,T}{T + 237.3}\right]
\label{eq:es}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Actual vapour pressure} (from mean relative humidity):
\begin{equation}
e_a = \frac{\text{RH}_{\text{mean}}}{100} \cdot e_s
\label{eq:ea}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Slope of the saturation vapour pressure curve:}
\begin{equation}
\Delta = \frac{4098 \cdot e^\circ(T_{\text{mean}})}{(T_{\text{mean}} + 237.3)^2}
\label{eq:slope}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Psychrometric constant:}
\begin{equation}
\gamma = 0.665 \times 10^{-3} \cdot P
\label{eq:gamma}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Net radiation:} The net shortwave radiation is $R_{ns} = (1 - \alpha)\,R_s$,
where $\alpha = 0.23$ is the grass reference surface albedo. The net radiation
is computed from the incident solar radiation with an empirical net longwave
approximation calibrated for the operational domain:

\begin{equation}
R_n = (1 - \alpha)\,R_s - 0.23\,R_s = 0.54\,R_s
\label{eq:rn}
\end{equation}

This simplified formulation, in which the net longwave radiation loss
($R_{nl}$) is approximated as $0.23\,R_s$, was adopted because the full
Stefan-Boltzmann net longwave calculation requires clear-sky solar radiation
($R_{so}$) and temperature-humidity corrections that introduce additional
uncertainty when computed from gridded data rather than station observations.
For the MATOPIBA validation domain (tropical, \mbox{5--12\textdegree{}S}), the
ratio $R_n/R_s \approx 0.50$--$0.58$ is relatively stable \citep{Allen1998},
and the simplified approximation ($R_n/R_s = 0.54$) introduces errors
of less than 5\% in net radiation for typical conditions.

\textbf{Extraterrestrial radiation} ($R_a$) is computed from latitude and day
of year following the standard astronomical formulations in
\citet{Allen1998}, incorporating the inverse relative Earth--Sun distance,
solar declination, and sunset hour angle.

\textbf{Quality classification.} Each daily ET\textsubscript{0} estimate is
classified as ``high'' quality when $0.1 \leq \text{ET}_0 \leq 15$~mm\,d\textsuperscript{$-1$},
and ``low'' otherwise, enabling downstream filtering for anomalous values.

\subsubsection*{Water deficit analysis}

EVAonline computes the daily water deficit as:

\begin{equation}
\text{WD} = K_c \cdot \text{ET}_0 - P_{\text{eff}}
\label{eq:water_deficit}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $K_c$ is the crop coefficient (user-defined or default
$K_c = 1.0$ for reference grass) and $P_{\text{eff}}$ is the effective
precipitation derived from the fused precipitation data. The irrigation
requirement is $\text{IR} = \max(0, \text{WD})$, and the cumulative deficit
over the analysis period provides an estimate of the total irrigation volume
required.


\subsection{Software Design and Operational Modes}
\label{subsec:software}

\subsubsection*{Operational modes}

EVAonline supports three operational modes that determine which data sources
are queried and how the fusion pipeline is configured:

\textbf{(a) Recent mode (7 days).} Queries all available sources for the most
recent 7-day period. This mode provides the most robust fusion with maximum
source redundancy. NASA POWER data may exhibit a 2--3 day latency, compensated
by real-time sources (Open-Meteo Forecast, MET Norway, NWS).

\textbf{(b) Historical mode (custom date range).} Queries NASA POWER
(1981--present) and Open-Meteo Archive with ERA5 reanalysis (1940--present)
for user-specified historical periods. This mode enables long-term
climatological analyses and trend detection. Results are delivered
asynchronously via email as CSV or Excel file attachments, accommodating the
longer processing times required for multi-year queries.

\textbf{(c) Forecast mode (7 days ahead).} Queries Open-Meteo Forecast, MET
Norway, and NWS Forecast (for US locations). This mode applies the
region-adaptive weights shown in Table~\ref{tab:weights}.

\subsubsection*{Asynchronous processing and real-time progress}

ET\textsubscript{0} calculations involve multiple sequential HTTP requests to
external climate APIs, data fusion computations, and database operations. To
prevent HTTP timeouts and provide a responsive user experience, all
calculations are processed asynchronously through the following pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The client submits a calculation request via HTTP POST.
\item The API creates an asynchronous Celery task and returns a unique task
  identifier (UUID) immediately.
\item The client establishes a WebSocket connection (via Redis pub/sub) to
  receive real-time progress updates.
\item The Celery worker executes the calculation pipeline, emitting progress
  events at each stage: elevation retrieval (10\%), climate data fetching
  (20--60\%, incremental per source), Kalman fusion (70\%), ET\textsubscript{0}
  computation (80\%), water deficit analysis (90\%), and completion (100\%).
\item The frontend renders a progress bar with step descriptions and displays
  the final results as interactive charts and tables.
\end{enumerate}

Failed tasks are retried up to three times with exponential backoff. The
system sends email notifications on both successful completion (with data file
attachment for historical mode) and on failure.

\subsubsection*{User interface}

The interactive dashboard provides: (a)~a Leaflet.js map for click-to-select
coordinate input with geocoding, or manual latitude/longitude entry with
real-time validation; (b)~mode selection (Recent, Historical, Forecast) with
date range pickers; (c)~WebSocket-driven progress bars during calculation;
(d)~interactive Plotly charts for daily ET\textsubscript{0} time series,
monthly summaries, source-by-source comparison, and cumulative water deficit;
(e)~per-component CSV, Excel, and PNG download buttons; and (f)~complete

system with session-based deduplication (Redis counters synchronized to

The API follows the OpenAPI~3.0 specification with automatic interactive

The validation protocol covers the 30-year period 1991--2020 (aligned with the
WMO standard climate normal period) and follows a seven-step procedure:
(1)~study area characterization with K\"oppen climate classification;
(2)~descriptive statistics of raw climate variables from each source;
(3)~data consolidation with standardized variable naming;
(4)~independent ET\textsubscript{0} calculation using NASA POWER only and
Open-Meteo Archive only;
(5)~cross-validation between individual-source ET\textsubscript{0} estimates;
(6)~execution of the complete two-stage fusion pipeline and comparison against
individual-source estimates; and
(7)~comprehensive multi-metric statistical comparison.

The reference dataset for validation is the Brazilian Daily Weather Gridded
Data (BR-DWGD) version~2 \citep{Xavier2016, Xavier2022}, which provides quality-controlled
daily meteorological data on a $0.1\text{\textdegree{}} \times 0.1\text{\textdegree{}}$
grid covering all of Brazil for the period 1961--2020.

Several key findings emerge from the validation analysis:

\textbf{Seasonal complementarity.} Performance improvement was most pronounced
during seasonal transitions (October--November and April--May in the Southern
Hemisphere), when inter-source variability is highest and the fusion provides
the greatest added value. During the dry season (May--September), NASA POWER
performed better than Open-Meteo for solar radiation, likely because lower
cloud cover reduces interference with satellite retrievals. Conversely,
Open-Meteo provided complementary information during the wet season, when
cloud cover degrades satellite-derived radiation estimates. The adaptive
Kalman filter effectively exploited this seasonal complementarity by adjusting
its innovation weighting according to the climatological context of each month.

\textbf{Radiation dominance.} Solar radiation ($R_s$) emerged as the most
critical variable for ET\textsubscript{0} accuracy. For historical periods,
NASA POWER's satellite-derived CERES radiation product was typically the only
available source with daily resolution, and its values entered the fusion at
100\% weight (single-source mode). For forecast and recent modes, multi-source
fusion of $R_s$ provided additional robustness.

\textbf{Climatological constraint benefit.} Sites within the 200~km
radius of a reference city (all 17 MATOPIBA sites) benefited from the
full adaptive Kalman filter with P01/P99 climatological bounds. The
adaptive measurement noise inflation effectively rejected anomalous values
that would have degraded the daily estimates. Sites falling back to the
simplified (fixed-parameter) Kalman filter showed less improvement,
underscoring the value of regional climatological priors.

\textbf{Bias reduction.} The fusion achieved a mean PBIAS of only 0.71\%,
compared to 3.2\% for NASA POWER alone and 5.8\% for Open-Meteo Archive.
This near-zero bias is particularly important for cumulative water balance
applications where systematic errors accumulate over time.

\subsection{Strengths, Limitations, and Future Directions}
\label{subsec:limitations}

\subsubsection*{Strengths}

The main strengths of EVAonline include: (a)~the two-stage fusion strategy
that exploits the complementary strengths of multiple data sources while
mitigating their individual weaknesses; (b)~the adaptive Kalman filter with
climatological priors that provides physically informed quality control beyond
simple averaging; (c)~the hexagonal architecture that enables straightforward
addition of new data sources through the standardized adapter interface;
(d)~the Docker-based deployment that eliminates installation complexity and
ensures reproducibility across environments; and (e)~the fully open-source
AGPL-3.0 license that enables community contributions, peer review, and
institutional deployment.

\subsubsection*{Limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, the simplified net radiation
approximation ($R_n \approx 0.54\,R_s$) introduces a systematic simplification
relative to the full FAO-56 net longwave radiation calculation; while
appropriate for the tropical MATOPIBA domain, this may introduce larger errors
in arid or high-latitude regions with different $R_n/R_s$ ratios. Second, the
climatological priors for the adaptive Kalman filter are currently available for
27 reference cities; locations beyond 200~km from any reference site fall back
to a simplified filter without climatological constraints, resulting in reduced
filtering effectiveness. Third, the platform currently relies exclusively on
gridded and reanalysis data products; integration of ground-based weather
station networks (e.g., INMET for Brazil, GHCN-Daily globally) would provide
additional independent observations to improve fusion quality. Fourth, the
ET\textsubscript{0} calculation is implemented as a scalar per-record
computation using Python's \texttt{math} module; while the data preprocessing
pipeline and Kalman filters leverage NumPy for array-level operations, fully
vectorizing the FAO-56 PM calculation would improve computational throughput
for large historical queries.

\subsubsection*{Future directions}

Priority areas for future development include: (a)~integration of ground-based
observation networks as additional fusion sources; (b)~expansion of the
climatological prior database from 27 to 200+ reference locations to achieve
near-global coverage with adaptive Kalman filtering; (c)~implementation of an
Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) that operates directly on the nonlinear FAO-56 PM
equation, fusing meteorological observations and ET\textsubscript{0} estimation
in a single step; (d)~machine learning-based bias correction layers trained on
regional station data; and (e)~development of ensemble-based uncertainty
quantification that provides confidence intervals for the final
ET\textsubscript{0} estimates, enabling risk-aware irrigation scheduling.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This paper presented EVAonline, an open-source, web-based platform for
reference evapotranspiration estimation that addresses the persistent challenge
of meteorological data scarcity and source-specific biases through multi-source
data fusion. The main contributions and findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Two-stage fusion methodology.} The combination of region-adaptive
weighted averaging (Stage~1) with an adaptive Kalman filter constrained by
30-year climatological priors (Stage~2) achieved a KGE of $0.814 \pm 0.053$
when validated against the BR-DWGD reference dataset at 17 MATOPIBA sites.
The fusion reduced RMSE by 26\% compared to the best individual source and
produced a near-zero bias (PBIAS~$= 0.71\%$), demonstrating that systematic
integration of complementary data sources yields measurably superior
ET\textsubscript{0} estimates.

\item \textbf{Accessibility.} By packaging the entire platform as
Docker-containerized microservices accessible through a web browser, EVAonline
eliminates technical barriers that have historically limited access to
calibrated ET\textsubscript{0} estimation. No local software installation,
programming knowledge, API keys, or data preprocessing are required.

\item \textbf{Extensibility.} The hexagonal architecture and standardized
adapter interface make it straightforward to integrate additional climate data
sources as they become available. The Kalman filter framework naturally
accommodates sources with different spatial resolutions, temporal coverages,
and quality levels.

\item \textbf{Reproducibility.} The complete source code (AGPL-3.0), Docker
deployment configuration, and independent validation dataset (published on
Zenodo) enable full reproduction of the results presented in this paper.
\end{enumerate}

EVAonline demonstrates that Kalman-filter-based data fusion of freely available
global climate data can provide reliable ET\textsubscript{0} estimates for
data-scarce agricultural regions, contributing to improved irrigation management
and water resource planning. The platform is particularly relevant for regions
such as MATOPIBA where agricultural expansion is outpacing the development of
meteorological observation infrastructure.
